Event hashtag - #ResBazWelli

The Wellington Research Bazaar is a free, two-and-a-half day conference promoting the digital literacy emerging at the centre of modern research. In the spirit of a marketplace or bazaar, ResBaz is a highly participatory event where researchers from many different disciplines can learn new skills, showcase current projects, and share knowledge.

Researchers at all levels and from all disciplines are encouraged to apply.
Research technologists who support researchers to do their job are welcome also.

The Wellington ResBaz will be broken into two key streams: Code and Learn. There's also a drop-in Make stream.

Code is comprised of Software Carpentry courses where you can get stuck into learning the basics of research computing;

Learn has various sessions to introduce a range of interesting programs, tools, and research tips and tricks;

Make will allow for hands-on interactions with a number of novel technologies.

There will be speakers from all stages of their research careers in various fields, as well as highlights on up-and-coming projects. The event is fully catered and will include a Data Olympics competition, Digital Research Awards, a beer and pizza evening — and of course valuable opportunities to share ideas and network.

Read more about the history and philosophy of ResBaz in these blog posts from The Research Whisperer and Software Carpentry.



CODE

(SOFTWARE CARPENTRY)

The Unix Shell

The Unix Shell allows you to do complex things with just a few keystrokes. More importantly, it helps them combine existing programs in new ways and automate repetitive tasks.

Programming with Python

The best way to learn how to program is to do something useful, so this introduction to Python is built around a common scientific task - data analysis. Our real goal isn’t to teach you Python, but to teach you the basic concepts that all programming depends on.

Version Control with Git

Version control is the lab notebook of the digital world. It’s what professionals use to keep track of what they’ve done and to collaborate with other people. Every large software development project relies on it, and most programmers use it for their small jobs as well. And it isn’t just for software - books, papers, small data sets, and anything that changes over time or needs to be shared can and should be stored in a version control system.

LEARN

(INTRODUCTORY ELECTIVES)

Quick Qualtrics

If data is the new gold, then survey-creation software Qaultrics maybe the gold digging tool you need. Learn how to capture both qualitative and quantitative data effectively by understanding survey design principles and tools. Sign up for an account here.

NVivo for Novices

Get an edge by better understanding the explosion of unstructured data in the world today using NVivo — powerful software for qualitative data analysis. VUW staff can download NVivo via the ITS Software Download Site.

Digital Storytelling Tool Suite

Overview of a range of free tools useful for digital history projects, including StoryMap, which fuses digital mapping with interactive storytelling. Possible assets to use here

Excel for Research

Microsoft Excel is widely used in a range of research contexts. This session will give some pointers on how to utilise it effeciently and effectively.

R for Novices

Introductory session outlining the basics of R, a free software environment for statistical computing and graphics

Know Thyself/Show Thyself

Promote your publications, increase impact and collaborate in research by learning about alternative metrics, social networking and unique author identifiers.

Weebly for Personal Websites

Learn how to create dynamic websites for research projects using Weebly

Learn LaTeX

LaTeX is a document preparation system for high-quality typesetting. It is most often used for medium-to-large technical or scientific documents but it can be used for almost any form of publishing.

Working with Wikipedia

Learn from Stuart Yeats about how you research outputs might best intersect with Wikipedia, and consider the question: when it comes to the world's largest reference site, is the truth relevant?
Related links:
Wikipedia Authority Control ; NZ Research ; Wikimedia Projects.

Visualising with Word Tree

Create dynamic visualisations of word associations and concordance to bring to life text-based research using Word Tree .

Meet Our Key Story Speakers

Diana Siwiak

VUW - Music/ECS

Diana Siwiak evolved from professional flutist to music engineer, then from music technologist to sonic artist and scientist. She educates and encourages anyone exuding enthusiasm to embrace the emerging fusion between technology and sonic art. Her long-term goal is to strengthen this intersection. To that end, Diana has immersed herself in several industry and academic settings in order to best understand and to create a bridge between the arts and engineering.

As a proficient musician and sonic scientist, Diana has a grasp of both the artistic and technical sides to music and sound. She is currently in her third year at Victoria University of Wellington as a Ph.D Candidate in Sonic Arts and Engineering, an interdisciplinary venture between the New Zealand School of Music and the School of Engineering. She investigates the application of auditory and gestural signal processing of a flutist's performance in order to quantify part of what makes for a musically expressive performance.

Michael Radich

VUW - Religious Studies

Broadly speaking, Michael works on the history of Buddhist ideas, and their reception in China. Over the past few years he has worked with developer Jamie Norrish to create TACL — a tool for performing analysis on text-based corpora, in particular the 188 million odd words of the Chinese Budhist Canon. Michael's taught himself ten natural languages — and most recently, Python.

Peter Ellis

MBIE

A multi-skilled data scientist, public servant, and overseas aid professional, Peter is a leader in the use of advanced analytics in New Zealand's public sector. His team is at the cutting edge of using open source software like R and Git to improve analytic workflow, efficiency and quality control in the spirit of reproducible research and literate programming.
